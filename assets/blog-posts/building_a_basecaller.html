Building a simple DNA basecaller in Python

I went to the Oxford Nanopore Community meetup 2022 to learn more about how the Oxford Nanopore sequencing devices work. One of the workshops I participated in educated us about the history of basecalling algorithms and had us try to build our own basecaller using sample raw data.

As a brief summary, nanopore sequencing is a technology that allows you to determine the sequences of DNA (and potentially RNA) in your sample of interest. How this works is that extracted DNA are prepped with reagents to make the DNA molecules nanopore sequencing compatible. The prepped sample is then loaded onto the sequencing device that contains a membrane full of biological pore proteins, termemd "nanopores", that can allow molecules to pass from one side of the membrane to the other. Samples are loaded alongside so-called "motor proteins" that bind to DNA molecules, tether themselves near a pore, and begin to feed the strand of DNA through the nanopore by unwinding the double-stranded and passing it into the pore as a single strand of DNA (in most cases).

Throughout this whole process, an electrical current is being applied through the membrane. As molecules pass through the pores, disruptions in the electrical current can be observed and measured.

This is where the concept of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) come in. HMMs are statistical models that make use of probabilistic transitions between hidden states to explain observed outputs. In the case of nanopore sequencing and DNA basecalling, the hidden states represent the unknown sequence of DNA bases, while the observed outputs correspond to the electrical signals measured by the sequencing device. Based on the observed electrical signals, we can design a program that estimates the probabilities of transitioning between different hidden states, and use this information to infer the most likely sequence of DNA bases that passed through the nanopore.

The premise of our project was simple, given a list of electrical readings at many different timepoints, decipher the sequence of DNA that went through the nanopore molecule. All we were given were three files to start with: 

signals.json ← A file containing electrical signal data obtained from a nanopore device

kmer_table.json ← A file containing a table each DNA dinucleotide and their corresponding electrical signature

sequences.json ← A file containing the correct sequences corresponding to the raw electrical data (to test the accuracy of the basecaller)

As you can see, the data provided in this k-mer table are for k-mers of length 2, also referred to as dinucleotides. The nanopores inside the sequencing device span the length of a couple of DNA bases and as so multiple bases are inside a nanopore protein at any given time as the strand of DNA is fed through the pore. Each permutation of DNA sequences will have a unique disruption pattern to the electrical current and therefore have a unique electrical signal that can be used to decode the sequence if these signatures are known beforehand (as in the table above). In real-life, many more bases can exist inside a nanopore at a given time than just 2 at once, and real life DNA bases can contain modifications that would need them to be treated as separate bases (termed Epigenetics). However, encoding all these permututations of k-mers would be too exhaustive for this challenge, as the goal of this problem is to get us to help us develop a conceptual understanding of what is going on at the molecular level within these sequencing devices and how various basecalling algorithms work and not so much to develop the most accurate basecalling algorithm possible. Therefore, a simplified dinucleotide table was provided.

I'll also create a way to group the signal data and the sequence data in order to keep things organized in my code and not have to remember which index I need to access corresponding data between my lists. I did this by creating a signal class whose objects contain the corresponding signals and correct sequences extracted using their index in the original lists. 

class Signal:

def __init__(self, signal_index):
self.signal_index = signal_index
self.signal = np.row_stack(signals[signal_index]) # Convert the signal data into a numpy array for easier slicing
self.correct_seq = sequencing[signal_index]

def __str__(self) -> str:
nl = "\n" # Trick I used to format newlines in f-strings
smoothed_signal = self.smoothed_signal if hasattr(self, "smoothed_signal") else "n/a"
return f"Signals array: {nl}{self.signal} {nl} {nl}Smoothed Signal: {nl}{smoothed_signal} {nl} {nl}Correct sequence: {nl}{self.correct_seq}"

At this resolution we can see a lot more clearly what may be happening as the DNA is going through the nanopore. How I like to conceptualize this is by breaking down the plot into 2 components: vertical-like elements and horizontal-like elements. Remember that inside the sequencer the DNA is being threaded through the nanopore by a motor protein. More specifically, the DNA is being ratcheted through the pore in a stepwise fashion. As the motor protein steps through and unwinds the DNA, there are periods where the DNA is let go of temporarilty, moves quickly through the nanopore channel, and then the motor protein stops the DNA. As the DNA is static, the sequencer will take a reading, and then take another reading, and take another reading, etc. until eventually the DNA will take a step. The DNA steps are random and it's driven by the dynamics of the motor protein. The "static phase" is therefore represented by the horzontal-like elements on the plot. These will be hereby known as "sequencing events" as they are the only time the DNA is stationary enough to provide an accurate reading. The vertical-like elements thereby represent the moments of time when the DNA is moving through the pore. This happens so quickly that there is no extractable sequence data from these timepoints.

Another thing you can notice at this high resolution is that the horizontal, sequencing events are a little jagged and not very smooth. This is "noise" that can be caused by a variety of events occuring inside the pore. The DNA molecule is not rigid inside the pore. It is moving around and displacing within the pore itself. Additionally, the motor protein is constantly undergoing conformation changes that if picked up by the nanopore can appear in the final signal.

Now that we have a good idea of what is going on on the molecular level in our data, we can begin to think about what steps we need to take to build the basecaller.

There are 2 big-picture approaches that can be implemented to tackle this challenge:

  1. We can filter the data at a sequence level by converting each signal from every timepoint into a corresponding dinucleotide and then trying to cut the sequence down to it's correct length
  2. We can filter the data at the signal level by trying to warp the signal in a way that would reduce incorrect sequencing calls and then perform the conversion using the k-mer table.

None of these strategies is the correct or incorrect one per-se, but the steps needed to implement these strategies differ greatly. Some people at the workshop attempted to build their basecallers using the first one, whereas others chose the second one. I personally opted to go with the second strategy because I liked the visual aid that the signal data provides. I have more of an intuition for when things are or aren't making sense looking at the signal plot compared to pure DNA code which would help me know if I'm on the right track.

Now that I have a strategy chosen, I'll begin to attempt to analyze the data.

The first challenge I have to tackle is to "smooth" the data. Data smoothing is a technique used in data analysis and data science to reduce the noise or variability in a dataset. It involves applying a mathematical algorithm to the data to remove fluctuations, outliers, or other sources of noise in the data. The purpose of data smoothing is to reveal underlying trends, patterns, or relationships in the data that may be obscured by random variations. There are different methods of data smoothing, but for this case whats important to find is any jumps in the electrical signal indicating the strand of DNA taking a "step" through the motor protein and introducing a new DNA base into the nanopore protein.

 def get_mean(signal, capture_ranges):    
  # Get the minimum and maximum timepoints for the capture range 
  minimum = capture_ranges[0]
  maximum = capture_ranges[-1]
  
  # If the minimum and maximum are different, take the mean of the signal between those timepoints
  if minimum != maximum:
    signal_means = np.mean(signal.signal[minimum:maximum])
    
  # If the minimum and maximum are the same, the mean is just the signal at that timepoint
  elif minimum == maximum:
    signal_means = signal.signal[minimum]
    
  # Return the mean signal value
  return signal_means

Now that we have the average current values, the next step is to find a way to determine which k-mer signature value it's closest to. One common way of performing this is to calculate the Euclidean distance between event and each kmer. The Euclidean distance measures the straight-line distance between two points in any-dimensional space (2-dimensions in this case). To compute the Euclidean distance, you calculate the squared difference between corresponding coordinates of the two points, sum up these squared differences across all dimensions, and take the square root of the result to obtain the final distance value. By finding the k-mer signature with the shortest euclidean distance to the average current value for a particular capture range, you can begin to basecall the DNA captured in the pore at that moment. 

# Write a function to calculate the euclidian distance between two values
def euclidian_distance(x,y):
  dist = np.sqrt(np.square((x - y)))
  return dist

# Write a function to basecall the dinucleotide based on the mean signal value
def basecall(signal, capture_ranges, kmer_table):
  # Get the mean signal value for the capture range
  capture_mean = get_mean(signal, capture_ranges)
  
  # Initialize an empty list to store the euclidian distances between the capture mean and each dinucleotide signature
  euc_distances = list()
  
  # Loop through each dinucleotide signature and calculate the euclidian distance between the capture mean and the signature
  for dinuc, signature in kmer_table.items():
    euc_distances.append((dinuc, euclidian_distance(capture_mean, signature)))
    
  # Get the minimum euclidian distance
  min_dist = np.min([x[1] for x in euc_distances])
  
  # Make a basecall
  dinuccall = [x[0] for x in euc_distances if x[1] == min_dist][0]
  
  # Return the basecall
  return dinuccall

We are almost done. You can see now that each capture point has the nucleotides that are present in the pore resolved. So at this stage it would seem intuitive to just collapse all the dinucleotides down to a single sequence and have that be the final sequence of DNA. However remember that the motor protein feeds the DNA through the pore one step (or one DNA base) at a time. Even though the pore can hold two basepairs (in this example), each base within the pore at adjacent capture periods is not unique. If you look at the graph, you can notice that the last DNA base at any capture event matches the first DNA base at the next capture event. This means that to get the final sequence, you have to only take last DNA base into account (the new one that just entered the pore), in order to not "double-count" bases. The only exception to this is the first capture event, where we can clearly see in these examples that it starts with both bases already in the pore. 

def collapse_seq(basecalls: "list") -> "str":
  """Collapse a list of basecalls into a single string"""
  
  # Initialize an empty string to store the final sequence
  final_seq = ""
  
  # Loop through each dinucleotide in the list of basecalls
  for i, v in enumerate(basecalls):
    
    # If it's the first basecall, add both bases to the final sequence
    if i == 0:
      final_seq += v
      continue
    
    # Otherwise, add the second base to the final sequence
    final_seq += v[-1]
    
  # Return the final sequence
  return final_seq

The first version of the basecaller was able to achieve an accuracy of 95%. Not a bad start. If you look closely at the sequence, you'll notice that the mismatches only occur at repetative sequences. This is a notorious problem for nanopore sequencing, known as "homopolymer indel errors". Homopolymers are repetitive sequences of the same nucleotide base (A, T, C, or G) within a DNA or RNA molecule. They can occur naturally or as a result of DNA or RNA synthesis or replication errors. For example, a homopolymer could consist of a repeated sequence of "A" nucleotides, such as "AAAAAA". The main challenge with homopolymers in nanopore sequencing arises from the difficulty in distinguishing the duration of time each nucleotide spends in the pore. Since each nucleotide in a homopolymer has the same base, the electrical signals produced by the pore can be similar or indistinguishable for consecutive nucleotides. As a result, the sequencing process may struggle to accurately determine the exact length or number of repeated nucleotides in a homopolymer region, and as a result insert or delete a DNA base at that sequence. To mitigate the challenges posed by homopolymers, various strategies are employed in nanopore sequencing, such as using specialized base-calling algorithms, employing specific sequencing chemistries or pore types that improve homopolymer resolution, and combining nanopore sequencing with other technologies for validation or error correction.

Now that we have a threshold point, we can try to adjust the capture timepoints factor in homopolymers. If we find suspected homopolymers in the signal, we can split up the capture ranges bases on how many homopolymers are suspected to be there. 

 def adjust_capture_timepoints(capture_timepoints, homopolymer_cutoff):
  # Make a copy of the capture timepoints to not modify the original argument
  capture_ranges = capture_timepoints.copy()

  # Loop through each capture range
  for i, capture_range in enumerate(capture_ranges):
  
    # If the capture range is long enough to be a suspected homopolymer
    if len(capture_range) >= homopolymer_cutoff:
      # Split the capture range in half
      split_point = int(len(capture_range) / 2)
      capture_ranges[i] = capture_range[:split_point]

      # Add the second half of the capture range to the next index in the list
      # If this range is also a suspected homopolymer, it will be split in the next iteration
      capture_ranges.insert(i+1, capture_range[split_point:])
    
  # Remove any empty lists that may have been created by the splitting
  capture_ranges = [x for x in capture_ranges if x != []]
  
  # Return the updated capture ranges
  return capture_ranges

def basecall_all(
  signals: "list", 
  kmer_signatures: "dict", 
  correct_seqs: "list", 
  movement_cutoff: "float" = 0.03, 
  homopolymer_cutoff: "int" = 20,
  detailed: "bool" = True) -> "list":
  
  # Initialize a list to store the accuracies of each signal
  accuracies = []
  
  # Loop through each signal in the list of signals
  for i, v in enumerate(signals):
    
    # Instantiate a new signal object
    current_signal = Signal(i)
    
    # Smooth the signal
    smoothed_signal = smooth_data(current_signal)

    # Set the smoothed signal as an attribute of the signal object
    setattr(current_signal, "smoothed_signal", smoothed_signal)
    
    # Get the timepoints where the DNA moves through the pore
    dna_movement_timepoints = get_movement_timepoints(current_signal, movement_cutoff)
    
    # Set the DNA capture timepoints as an attribute of the signal object
    setattr(current_signal, "movement_timepoints", dna_movement_timepoints)
    
    # Get the timepoints where the DNA is captured in the pore
    dna_capture_timepoints = get_capture_timepoints(current_signal, movement_cutoff)

    # Adjust the DNA movement timepoints to account for homopolymers
    dna_capture_timepoints = adjust_capture_timepoints(dna_capture_timepoints, homopolymer_cutoff)
    
    # Set the DNA capture timepoints as an attribute of the signal object
    setattr(current_signal, "capture_timepoints", dna_capture_timepoints)
    
    # Basecall the signal
    basecalls_list = basecall_signal(current_signal, current_signal.capture_timepoints, kmer_signatures)
    
    # Collapse the basecalls into a single sequence
    final_sequence = collapse_seq(basecalls_list)
    
    # Set the basecalled sequence as an attribute of the signal object
    setattr(current_signal, "basecalled_seq", final_sequence)
    
    # Align the basecalled sequence to the correct sequence
    alignment, nice_alignment = align(current_signal.basecalled_seq, current_signal.correct_seq)
    
    # Calculate the accuracy of the basecaller
    accuracy = measure_accuracy(alignment, current_signal.correct_seq)
    accuracies.append(accuracy)
    
    # Print out the alignment

      print(f"The alignment for signal {i + 1} is:")
      print("basecalled: " + nice_alignment['query_aligned'])
      print("            " + nice_alignment['matched_aligned'])
      print("   correct: " + nice_alignment['target_aligned'])
      print(f"The accuracy for this sequence is: {accuracy}%")
      print("\n")

  basecaller_accuracy = sum(accuracies) / len(accuracies) if len(accuracies) > 0 else 0
  print(f"Finished basecalling {len(signals)} signals")
  print(f"The average accuracy of the basecaller is: {basecaller_accuracy}%")

# Define the Signal class
class Signal:

  def __init__(self, signal_index):
    self.signal_index = signal_index
    self.signal = np.row_stack(signals[signal_index]) 
    self.correct_seq = sequences[signal_index]

  def __str__(self) -> str:

def smooth_data(signal):
  # Subtract the first element from the second, the second from the third, and so on. Get the absolute value of the result
  smoothed_signal = np.abs(np.array(signal.signal[0:len(signal.signal) - 1]) - np.array(signal.signal[1:len(signal.signal)]))

  # Return the smoothed signal
  return smoothed_signal


def get_movement_timepoints(signal, cutoff):
  # Checks each timepoint and assigns it boolean value if value is greater than the cutoff 
  timepoints_bool = signal.smoothed_signal > cutoff

  # Grab the indicies of the timepoints where the value was greater than the cutoff
  dna_movement_timepoints = np.array([i for i, x in enumerate(timepoints_bool) if x])

  # Insert a timepoint 0 at the beginning of the array to represent the inital DNA that was present
  dna_movement_timepoints = np.insert(dna_movement_timepoints, 0, 0)

  # Output the timepoints
  return dna_movement_timepoints


def get_capture_timepoints(signal, cutoff):
  # Reinitialize the list to store the timepoints where the DNA was captured
  dna_capture_timepoints = list()

  # Loop through each timepoint and calculate the mean of the signal values between each timepoint
  for i in range(0,len(signal.movement_timepoints)-1):
    
    """Modify the miniminum and maximum values to make the capture range a bit shorter
    Add a little less buffer to the very start to capture more of the initial capture event""" 
    minimum_buffer = 2
    maximum_buffer = 0
    minimum = signal.movement_timepoints[i] + 1 if i == 0 else signal.movement_timepoints[i] + minimum_buffer
    maximum = signal.movement_timepoints[(i+1)] - maximum_buffer
    
    # Append the range of timepoints to the list of capture timepoints
    dna_capture_timepoints.append(list(range(minimum,maximum)))
    
  # Return the list of timepoints
  return dna_capture_timepoints


def adjust_capture_timepoints(capture_timepoints, homopolymer_cutoff):
  # Make a copy of the capture timepoints to not modify the original argument
  capture_ranges = capture_timepoints.copy()

  # Loop through each capture range
  for i, capture_range in enumerate(capture_ranges):
  
    # If the capture range is long enough to be a suspected homopolymer
    if len(capture_range) >= homopolymer_cutoff:
      # Split the capture range in half
      split_point = int(len(capture_range) / 2)
      capture_ranges[i] = capture_range[:split_point]

      # Add the second half of the capture range to the next index in the list
      # If this range is also a suspected homopolymer, it will be split in the next iteration
      capture_ranges.insert(i+1, capture_range[split_point:])
    
  # Remove any empty lists that may have been created by the splitting
  capture_ranges = [x for x in capture_ranges if x != []]
  
  # Return the updated capture ranges
  return capture_ranges


def get_mean(signal, capture_ranges):    
  # Get the minimum and maximum timepoints for the capture range 
  minimum = capture_ranges[0]
  maximum = capture_ranges[-1]
  
  # If the minimum and maximum are different, take the mean of the signal between those timepoints
  if minimum != maximum:
    signal_means = np.mean(signal.signal[minimum:maximum])
    
  # If the minimum and maximum are the same, the mean is just the signal at that timepoint
  elif minimum == maximum:
    signal_means = signal.signal[minimum]
    
  # Return the mean signal value
  return signal_means


def get_capture_means (signal, capture_ranges):
  # Initialize a list to store the mean signal values
  dna_capture_means = list()

  # Loop through each capture range and calculate the mean of the signal values between each timepoint
  for i in capture_ranges:
    dna_capture_means.append(get_mean(signal, i))
  
  # Return the list of mean signal values
  return dna_capture_means


def euclidian_distance(x,y):
  dist = np.sqrt(np.square((x - y)))
  return dist


def basecall(signal, capture_ranges, kmer_table):
  # Get the mean signal value for the capture range
  capture_mean = get_mean(signal, capture_ranges)
  
  # Initialize an empty list to store the euclidian distances between the capture mean and each dinucleotide signature
  euc_distances = list()
  
  # Loop through each dinucleotide signature and calculate the euclidian distance between the capture mean and the signature
  for dinuc, signature in kmer_table.items():
    euc_distances.append((dinuc, euclidian_distance(capture_mean, signature)))
    
  # Get the minimum euclidian distance
  min_dist = np.min([x[1] for x in euc_distances])
  
  # Make a basecall
  dinuccall = [x[0] for x in euc_distances if x[1] == min_dist][0]
  
  # Return the basecall
  return dinuccall


def basecall_signal(signal, capture_ranges, kmer_table):
  # Initialize a list to store the basecalls
  basecalls = []

  # Loop through each capture range and basecall each capture range
  for cap_range in capture_ranges:
    basecalls.append(basecall(signal, cap_range, kmer_table))
  
  # Return the list of basecalls
  return basecalls


def collapse_seq(basecalls: "list") -> "str":
  """Collapse a list of basecalls into a single string"""
  
  # Initialize an empty string to store the final sequence
  final_seq = ""
  
  # Loop through each dinucleotide in the list of basecalls
  for i, v in enumerate(basecalls):

    # If it's the first basecall, add both bases to the final sequence
    if i == 0:
      final_seq += v
      continue

    # Otherwise, add the second base to the final sequence
    final_seq += v[-1]

  # Return the final sequence
  return final_seq


def align(basecalled_seq: "str", correct_seq: "str") -> "dict":
  """Align two sequences using edlib"""
  
  # Perform the alignment
  align_result = edlib.align(basecalled_seq, correct_seq, task='path')
  
  # Format the alignment result
  nice_alignment = edlib.getNiceAlignment(align_result, basecalled_seq, correct_seq)
  
  # Return the alignment result
  # print(align_result)
  return align_result, nice_alignment


def measure_accuracy (alignment: "dict", correct_seq: "str") -> "float":
  acc = (1 - alignment['editDistance'] / len(correct_seq)) * 100  
  return acc


# Putting it all together
def basecall_all(
  signals: "list", 
  kmer_signatures: "dict", 
  correct_seqs: "list", 
  movement_cutoff: "float" = 0.03, 
  homopolymer_cutoff: "int" = 20,
  detailed: "bool" = True) -> "list":
  
  # Initialize a list to store the accuracies of each signal
  accuracies = []
  
  # Loop through each signal in the list of signals
  for i, v in enumerate(signals):
    
    # Instantiate a new signal object
    current_signal = Signal(i)
    
    # Smooth the signal
    smoothed_signal = smooth_data(current_signal)

    # Set the smoothed signal as an attribute of the signal object
    setattr(current_signal, "smoothed_signal", smoothed_signal)
    
    # Get the timepoints where the DNA moves through the pore
    dna_movement_timepoints = get_movement_timepoints(current_signal, movement_cutoff)
    
    # Set the DNA capture timepoints as an attribute of the signal object
    setattr(current_signal, "movement_timepoints", dna_movement_timepoints)
    
    # Get the timepoints where the DNA is captured in the pore
    dna_capture_timepoints = get_capture_timepoints(current_signal, movement_cutoff)

    # Adjust the DNA movement timepoints to account for homopolymers
    dna_capture_timepoints = adjust_capture_timepoints(dna_capture_timepoints, homopolymer_cutoff)
    
    # Set the DNA capture timepoints as an attribute of the signal object
    setattr(current_signal, "capture_timepoints", dna_capture_timepoints)
    
    # Basecall the signal
    basecalls_list = basecall_signal(current_signal, current_signal.capture_timepoints, kmer_signatures)
    
    # Collapse the basecalls into a single sequence
    final_sequence = collapse_seq(basecalls_list)
    
    # Set the basecalled sequence as an attribute of the signal object
    setattr(current_signal, "basecalled_seq", final_sequence)
    
    # Align the basecalled sequence to the correct sequence
    alignment, nice_alignment = align(current_signal.basecalled_seq, current_signal.correct_seq)
    
    # Calculate the accuracy of the basecaller
    accuracy = measure_accuracy(alignment, current_signal.correct_seq)
    accuracies.append(accuracy)
    
    # Print out the alignment
    if detailed:
      print(f"Basecalling signal {i+1} out of {len(signals)}")
      print(f"The alignment for signal {i + 1} is:")
      print("basecalled: " + nice_alignment['query_aligned'])
      print("            " + nice_alignment['matched_aligned'])
      print("   correct: " + nice_alignment['target_aligned'])
      print(f"The accuracy for this sequence is: {accuracy}%")
      print("\n")
      
  # Determine the average accuracy of the basecaller
  basecaller_accuracy = sum(accuracies) / len(accuracies) if len(accuracies) > 0 else 0
  print(f"Finished basecalling {len(signals)} signals")
  print(f"The average accuracy of the basecaller is: {basecaller_accuracy}%")
  

# Main function entry point
