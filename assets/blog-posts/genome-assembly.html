Background Information on Modern Genome Sequencing

A little background on genome assembly technologies before I start with the workshop portion. The first full DNA genome to be sequenced was that of bacteriophage φX174 in 1977. Since then, it took researchers until 2022 to create a fully-resolved, human genome assembly. This breakthrough was powered by innovations in genome sequencing technologies that overcame decades long challenges in genomics.

The main hurdles to creating contiguous genome assemblies have been, and to this day still is, dealing with repetative regions in the genome.

These regions confuse assembly algorithms and lead to fragmented or misassembled genomes. In order to effectively resolve repetative regions in the genome, you need sequencing reads that are longer than the span of the repetative regions, and in high-enough abundance to resolve any ambiguities in the assembly algorithms.

Modern sequencing technologies, also known as long-read technologies, capture much more of the genome per-read, and have enabled us resolve a lot of tricky genomic areas, and investigate genetic phenomena across the tree of life that previously was never possible. Some interesting patterns were noticed. Firstly, the human genome contains around 26,000 genes. This number is pretty consistent across the tree of life. It's become evident that a lot of the genetic diversity that we see comes at the regulatory, or epigenomic level. Another interesting phenomenon that emerged across biodiversity genomics studies is something known as the C-value paradox. The C-value refers to the amount of DNA in the haploid genome of an organism. Very similar organisms can show a large difference in C-value (e.g. Flowering plants and amphibians).

Assemblies using long-read technologies also improve the quality control process, reduce computational intesity for assembly algorithms, and reduce the coverage-demands per region of the genomes.

One of the current long-read sequencing providers is Pacific Biosciences. As a high-level overview, their technology works by capturing single molecules of DNA in small pores and polymerizing a complimentary strand of DNA with flourescent DNA bases.

The main problem with this technology is that the DNA polymerase can synthesize the complementary strand at a rate of 1000 base pairs per second. This rate can actually be too fast sometimes for the machine to measure the fluorescence accurately at the same rate and so it might miss some insertions or get confused. This means that the main error type for this technology are deletions and insertions (indels).

The first generation of this technology was called PacBio continuous long reads, or PacBio CLR reads. These reads were very errorneous, and could have a per-read error rate of around 15%. This is a problem in the assembly process because most assembly algorithms require you to have good quality reads and so you had to correct your reads before assembly. This is computationally a very costly process but also causes you to lose a lot of your data afterwards. Noisy reads also pose problems for certain hard to assemble regions, especially if you don't have even sequencing coverage across the genome.

The next generation of reads were called PacBio Circular Consensus Reads, also known as PacBio HiFi reads. These are the current gold-standard reads for performing genome assembly.

The innovation here is that the DNA sequence is circularized with special sequencing adapters and the molecule is read over-and-over again. Because the errors in each pass of the sequencing should be randomly distributed accross the reads, this strategy will give you enough sequencing depth and every base in the sequence to create an accurate consensus sequence, which in most cases is extremely close to the reality of what the original sequence was.

The other major player in the long-read sequencing space is Oxford Nanopore Technologies sequencing. These technologies work on the native molecule of DNA, sequencing it by analysing the electrical signal that is disrupted as the DNA molecular passes through a pore in a membrane.

This technology produces the longest DNA sequencing reads out of any technology on the market, with the maximum read length only restricted by the input fragment size. Typical read length is around 10,000 base pairs, but this can very significaly depending on which strategy you choose during preparation of the sequencing library.

The biggest downside of this technology is the poor quality per-read. For genome assembly, you want the highest-accuracy reads you can find. The trade-off for higher read lengths in Nanopore sequencing is usually not worth it. These reads are instead useful for resolving long repetative regions that can't be resolved by PacBio HiFi reads.

Recently, there have been two technolgies that have been released to correct Nanopore reads after they've been basecalled, called HERRO and DeChat. These technologies are promising, but each still have enough problems (overcorrection, loss of coverage, and potentially-introduced AI bias) where making the decision to just use pure Nanopore sequencing for a genome assembly project is not yet recommended. PacBio HiFi reads are still more accurate and still reign king.

The first thing I wanted to do was to perform a k-mer analysis on the sequencing reads for this organism.

In biology, k-mers are unique subsequences of a sequence of length k.

Performing a k-mer analysis is a powerful tool in genomics with several key applications. It plays a fundamental role in genome assembly by enabling the construction of De Bruijn graphs, which help reconstruct sequences from short reads through overlap identification (although this assembly method is less in-favour these days due to modern sequencing technologies whose reads assemble better using the other main approach to genome assembly, the Overlap, Layout, Consensus (OLC) method).

Additionally, k-mer frequency analysis can detect bacterial contamination in eukaryotic genome assemblies by revealing discrepancies in CG content.

Here you can see two clusters with different GC contents. If your sequencing reads are of high enough quality you can perform a contamination analysis from just the read data alone.

k-mer analysis is also useful for correcting errors in next-generation sequencing (NGS) data by identifying abnormal k-mer distributions. Moreover, k-mer compositions across different genomes can indicate horizontal gene transfer events by highlighting unusual patterns. This method is also useful for aiding in locating CpG islands, which are regions rich in cytosine and guanine dinucleotides often associated with gene regulation. All these features and more make k-mer analysis a versatile and essential tool in genomic research. More information about k-mer counting can be found in this blogpost by Bernardo J. Clavijo, one of the authors of the k-mer counting program KAT, or in this recently submitted guide to performing k-mer analyses.

For our purposes here, we'll explore two other major roles of k-mer analysis that are vital to genome assembly — especially de novo genome assembly — the ability to estimate genome size and heterozygosity, which are a major factor in estimating the completeness of a genome assembly.

Now it's time to determine what size to pick for k. Choosing k is a tradeoff between specificity and sensitivity. Using a k that is too small will result in many unrelated sequences being composed of the same k-mers, in a textbook case of specificity loss because there being very few possible k-mers of that size. In the extreme of the small k, k=1 only distinguishes two canonical k-mers: A and C. 1-mer analysis is incredibly popular in biology, but it is best known by the name of GC content analysis. Using extremely large k values would sacrifice many of the benefits and sensitivity of k-mer analyses in the first place. For the k-mer size, I picked 31. This is a common k-mer size in genomic analysis because it provides a good balance between specificity and sensitivit. It is long enough to provide specific sequences that help reduce ambiguity (like distinguishing between similar regions, and between mammalian and bacterial DNA) while still being short enough to maintain a high frequency in the data. It is also computationally efficient. While longer k-mers can increase the computational burden due to the larger number of possible combinations, 31 is often a manageable size that balances memory resources and processing effectiveness when analyizing large amounds of DNA bases.

I also had to make sure that --canonical was chosen.

DNA is typically double-stranded. When sequencing, we can read from either strand. However, we would like to consider every location of the genome once, no matter on which strand we happened to have landed.

For example, if we read the sequence ATCGAC, it indicates that both this sequence and its reverse complement, GTCGAT, exist in the genome. One is observed when reading in one direction, while the other is observed in the opposite direction. To ensure completeness in our analyses and to prevent over-counting of k-mers across the genome, we should take both of these sequences into account as a single k-mer, ATCGAC/GTCGAT.

To standardize how we refer to k-mers, we can use the concept of canonical k-mers. This means selecting the lexicographically smaller of the two reverse complementary sequences, which helps us consistently represent these sequences. Essentially, if we have both ATCGAC and GTCGAT, we only refer to ATCGAC, as it comes first in alphabetical order.

Most k-mer counting tools either default to counting canonical k-mers or offer an option to switch between canonical and non-canonical counting. Typically, canonical counting is preferred, meaning that if GTCGAT appears, it will be counted as ATCGAC. However jellyfish by default does not use canonical k-mers, and so this option has to be specified.

size and threads were also specified, but this has more to do with computational efficiency and computer memory management than any biologically relevant parameters.

jellyfish count --mer-len=31 --size=1000 --threads=1 --canonical --output=drUrtUren1.ccs.total.jf drUrtUren1.ccs.total.fasta.gz

Now that a histogram has been generated of these k-mer counts, the next step is to visualize it. To do this, GenomeScope 2.0 was used. This program enables you to estimate the size, heterozygosity, and repeat content of the genome based on the frequency of k-mers found in the sequencing data. It comes with the web application accessible at http://genomescope.org/genomescope2.0/ and so the data was pulled off the workshop server first and then uploaded to this website.

GenomeScope analyzes the k-mer profile (also called k-mer spectrum) of unassembled, unaligned (i.e. raw) reads to compute the genome and read characteristics. The k-mer profile counts how many k-mers, substrings of length k, occur in the sequence data. A key advantage of the k-mer based approach is that the k-mer profile can be computed without a reference genome by directly scanning the sequencing reads, so that it works well for novel genomes. The frequency of unique k-mers are plotted against the k-mer coverage, the formula of which is shown here:

So plotted out, a homozygous genome — free of repeats and free of sequencing errors — should be distributed around this location on a k-mer spectrum plot:

If instead the genome is heterozygous, then the k-mer profile will show a characteristic two-peak profile. The two peaks will be centered at X and 2X (e.g. 50x and 100x) representing heterozygous k-mers that have been sequenced to half the coverage (50x) as the homozygous k-mers sequenced equally from both alleles (100x).

The relative heights of the peaks will be determined by the overall rate of heterozygosity: low heterozygosity causes the first peak to be relatively short and higher rates of heterozygosity causes the first peak to grow taller and taller. Since each heterozygous base can create 2 × k heterozygous k-mers, the height of the heterozygous peak grows very quickly and matches the height of the homozygous peak at around only 1.2% heterozygosity for k=21.

Repeats have the opposite effect and cause k-mers to be present more than 2X times, proportional to the number of copies of that repeat in the genome.

Sequencing errors and read duplications distort the distribution by creating false k-mers that only occur a few numbers of times or artificially creating duplicated k-mers, which reduces the effective average coverage and increases the variance of the distributions. As with heterozygous variation, since each erroneous base can create 2 × k erroneous k-mers, the height of the erroneousus peak grows very quickly. However because these errors will be scattered randomly across the reads unlike heterozygous variation, these k-mers' peak will be differentiable from the heterozygous peaks because off the much smaller amount of genome coverage.

Step 2: Performing a Genome Assembly

In order to perform a genome assembly, you have to pick an ideal assembler for your data. This involves understanding the basic mechanisms behind how these assemblers work and also the differences between the popular assemblers used by the research community. This is critical because different assemblers employ different overall strategies and are designed to assemble reads that span the genome at a certain coverage, and depending on which one you choose will effect how you interpret and process your data downstream of the initial assembly step.

For diploid organisms, a lot of modern long, high-quality read assemblers will work by first creating a collapsed draft assembly, called the primary assembly (usually a fusion of the paternal and maternal haplotypes). This assembly is as contiguous as possible without scaffolding. Then, an alternate assembly will try to be assembled. This assembly will usually be much more fragmented, because it is usually comprised of all the heterozygous variation not collapsed into the primary assembly. The primary assembly will contain all the homozygous sequences common to both maternal and paternal chromosomes (see the left side of the figure below for reference).

For PacBio HiFi data, the current consensus best assembler is the hifiasm assembler written by Heng Li (see paper here). This was verified by a benchmarking study published in 2024, however it's always important to stay up to date with the literature and the latest trends and understand the biology of your organism before making your decision of which assembler to choose for your organism.

hifiasm works by first correcting errors in the sequencing reads if there are 3 or more reads that overlap at an errorneous location. It then tries to reconstruct both haplotypes into a primary and an alternate assembly using an internal purging tool. It can then phase these assemblies into their correct paternal and maternal chromosomes if complimentary data (either trio binning data or Hi-C data is provided). Trio binning involves taking illumina sequencing reads from the parents of the organism and using that data to resolve the haplotypes of the child's genome assembly. This method is useful but it doesn't work very accurately if the paternal, maternal, and child genomes are too homozygous. Hi-C on the other hand is an assay that uses DNA-DNA proximity ligation to identify long range chromatin contacts in an unbiased, genome-wide fashion. If a high quality Hi-C library is generated, it can be used to phase the genome of the organism (elaborated on in a further section of this post).

HiCanu was one of the first generation of HiFi read assemblers. HiCanu also does read error correction in the approach as hifiasm. It finds the coverage in the reads and if there is enough of a consensus overlap to correctly identify an error it will correct it. However one major difference in it's assembly strategy is it performs homopolymer compression (e.g. CAAAT -> CAT). This is done because when you have a homopolymer in the sequence, there is a higher chance that you will have errors in the sequencing reads. So through this strategy, the OLC graph is created using compressed reads that likely have less errors and then after the assembly graph is created, it is expanded to retain the homopolymer information. Also, because HiFi reads are very long and high quality, the assembler can detect and seperate haplotypes very well. HiCanu tries to find perfect overlaps, so when it finds something like a SNP, it's probably going to thinks it's another haplotype of the same contig. So HiCanu is going to output to you a pot of contigs with double the expected genome size. It combines all the haplotype contigs into one output files. This is why you have to run a post processing step on HiCanu assemlbies especially (using tools like purge_dups)

Remember that assemblers work at a specific coverage. According to the documentation, HiCanu will work best around 30 - 60x coverage. This is important to keep in mind because lets say for example that we were trying to assemble an organellar genome like the mitochondrion. Reads from mitochondria might comprise an extremely high amount of the DNA in the sample, and you could get upwards of 500X coverage for this small genome. If you were to try to use a program like HiCanu to assemble this, HiCanu would not take all 500X reads into account to try to assemble this genome, especially since for HiCanu you need to provide an estimated genome size. 600X coverage for a small genome is overkill and so it would try to downsample the reads to get it to a more optimal coverage range and this downsampling would be random. This means that running the same assembly program with the same input could result in 2 different assemblies if you are not aware of this behaviour. Once again, it's important to pick the right tool for the job. Organelle genome assemblies should be performed using programs like MitoHiFi or Oatk.

hifiasm doesn't require you to provide the estimated genome size. All it requires is the sequencing reads for assembly. The --primary flag tells hifiasm to output both a primary and an alternate assembly, with emphasis on generating a more contiguous primary assembly containing homozygous and some heterzygous regions. The -f0 flag is used to disable the initial bloom filter which takes 16 GB of memory at the beginning. The bloom filter is used to filter out singleton k-mers when counting all k-mers. It takes 2 ( INT - 3 ) bytes of memory. Using a proper value for -f saves memory. -f37 is recommended for human assemblies, and for genomes much larger than human, applying -f38 or even -f39 is preferred to save memory on k-mer counting. However for small genomes it's often recommended to disable this with -f0.

Step 3: Genome Assembly Quality Control

Now that we have assemblies generated for these organisms, the next question is how do we start verifying whether these assemblies are of high quality. An organism's genomes will often contain hundreds and thousands of A's, C's, G's, and T's seemingly randomly scrambled together in one sequence. Trying to find out whether these sequences are arranged in the correct order or if there are any sequences missing is a challenge that involves clever algorithms and use of existing data in biological databases like the NCBI. A few common tactics for genome assembly quality checking are:

  1. Checking for Base Accuracy

    • This can be done by realigning reads from the same species' k-mers . This involves aligning sequencing reads based on k-mers that are specific to the target species. The goal is to minimize errors and ensure that the assembly accurately represents the species' genomic sequence. This step can help identify misassemblies or regions of low accuracy in the genome.
    • Another method is by checking for k-mer Completeness with Respect to Illumina or HiFi Data. This involves comparing the k-mer distribution from the assembled genome against the k-mers obtained from high-quality sequencing data (e.g., Illumina or HiFi reads). This comparison helps determine how well the assembly captures the genetic diversity and completeness of the genome, identifying regions that may be missing or poorly represented.

  2. Assessing Local Structure Accuracy

    • This can be done by using external evidence, which refers to incorporating additional sequencing data (such as long-read or mate-pair data) to validate the structure of the assembly. External evidence can help correct misalignments and fill gaps, enhancing the overall accuracy of the assembled genome.
    • It can also be done by using Known Adjacencies, which involves utilizing information from known genomic features, such as PCR primer locations or previously identified structural variants, to improve local structure accuracy. This approach helps verify the order and orientation of genes and other genomic elements, ensuring that the assembly reflects the expected genomic architecture.

  3. Analyzing Gene Content and Order and Orientation of Genes/Exons of Known Genes

    • Analyzing the arrangement of genes and exons within the assembled genome is crucial for functional annotation.
    • Comparing the gene order and orientation against a reference or known gene set (e.g., housekeeping genes) can reveal structural integrity and evolutionary conservation. This information is vital for understanding gene regulation and potential gene interactions.

  4. Quantitative Assessment of Genome Assembly and Annotation Completeness

    • The gold standard for performing a quantitative assessment on genome annotation has for the longest time been BUSCO (Benchmarking Universal Single-Copy Orthologs). BUSCO provides a standardized method for evaluating genome assemblies based on the presence and completeness of conserved genes across different species.
    • By assessing the assembly against a set of expected single-copy orthologs, BUSCO quantifies the completeness of both the genome assembly and its annotation. This tool helps you determine how well the assembly meets evolutionary expectations and informs decisions on further refinements or analyses.
    • Basically, it works by starting from a set of conserved genes from a species lineage, and then finding them in the assembly and assessing whether they are complete (C), single copy (S), duplicated (D), fragmented (F), or missing (M).
    • This thus means that the accuracy of the BUSCO measurement is dependent on how accurate the data is in the lineage databases.
    • Some newer projects are being built that try to improve on the accuracy of BUSCO, one of these being a faster and more accurate re-implementation of BUSCO called compleasm.

Now that the mapping has finished, I then used a tool called samtools to manipulate the output file. When the mapping has completed, the information is stored inside a .sam file. This .sam file is human readable but it is also very large and takes up a lot of storage space. When working with reads, it's more common to work with .bam files. These files are not human readable (they are binary files and just contain a series of 0's and 1's) but they store the same information as the .sam files and consume much less memory. It's more common to store alignment data as .bam files and use tools like samtools to manipulate and read these files. Another thing that is often done to these alignment files is to index them. Step 4: Purging Duplications and Merqury evaluation

Now that the genomes are assembled, the next required step will be to remove any sort of duplications from the genome. Genome assemblies that don't perform this step are often inaccurate and can lead to a lot of incorrect biological insights. An interesting example that demonstrates this well came from a study that analyzed genomes generated by the Vertabrate Genomes Project and other researchers. The Vertebrate Genomes Project is an initiative aimed at producing high-quality reference genomes for a wide range of vertebrate species. The project focuses on generating genome assemblies that are both comprehensive and accurate utilizing the most modern and advanced sequencing technologies. While most of their genome assemblies are performed de novo, recently this project created assemblies of three species that already contained reference assemblies on NCBI, those being the platypus, zebra finch, and Anna’s hummingbird. Some researcher decided to compare the older genome assemblies with the new ones generated by the Vertebrate Genomes Project, which used methods like haplotype phasing and purging to reduce false duplications. By conducting whole genome alignments, they identified the extent of false duplications, finding that 4 to 16% of sequences in the previous assemblies were inaccurately duplicated, leading to the overestimation of gene family expansions.

The main source of these false duplications is what's known as heterotype duplications, where certain haplotype sequences were relatively more divergent than other parts of the genome leading the assembly algorithms to classify them as separate genes or genomic regions (false paralogs). However, there are two other types of errors that are quite important. Switch errors occur in which variants from each haplotype are assembled into the same pseudo-haplotype. You will basically have a hybrid between the maternal and the paternal haplotype. You don't even have necessarily the heterozygosity belonging to only one of them. Also nucleotide consensus errors occur due to the collapse between the two haplotypes, causing mixing of SNPs and of different variants from both haplotypes. To fix this, you need to do some post-processing of the initial assemblies.

What happens routinely during genome assembly without phasing, is that two haplotypes that belong to the same region but are somehow divergent, both end up in one of the assemblies.

They end up being introduced into one contig with a gap in the middle (kind of like they are scaffolded together, as some assembly algorithms try to perform scaffolding after contigging and this is an artifact of that).

You end up with sequences that should not be in the primary assembly, but yet they are. Therefore, often times, your primary assemblies before purging will be larger than what you expect in your final asssembly. This is a very common problem when assembling using poor quality reads like PacBio CLR, but still happens even in HiFi reads.

So how can you identify whether this has happened in your assemblies at this stage?

One thing you can do is map your reads back to your genome. This will produce a coverage plot. If you have this duplication in, for example a 2n genome, you will expect to see a drop in coverage in the reads to about half this level at the location where the read occurs. Also, you'll likely see a spot with 0 coverage in the middle. This coverage signature is a good indication that something like this has occured. Assemblers will often perform this kind of test periodically during assembly to try to find and remove these errors, but they aren't perfect and so often times you'll find these errors in the primary assembly.

If you see a coverage signature similar to the one below, then there is a good chance that your assembly contains an erroneous insertion. However, be aware that performing sequence alignment with improper alignment parameters can also cause reads from 2 similar sequences to pileup in a single region and produce a similar pattern. Often times if this step is done as part of a post-processing program, the alignment parameters chosen by the program should be sufficient to prevent this kind of thing, but it is good to be aware about and these regions might need closer inspection.

But then after you have found these duplication patterns from the read mapping back to the assembly, how can you ensure that regions that drop in coverage are actually false duplications. You can perform a self-alignment. If these regions are actually similar, you can conclude that they are probably these kind of false duplication assembly artifacts and can safetly remove them. This is essentially what the program purge_dups does.

In more detail, purge_dups works as follows: after you run your assembler and you have the contigs of the primary assembly, if you believe the size of this assembly is larger than your true genome size and you suspect haplotype retention, you run purge_dups. purge_dups will map all your reads back to your assembly, and it's going to find an even coverage (aka average coverage), for all the reads in your assembly, in this example that number is 8x.

purge_dups will identify retained haplotigs by looking for parts of the genome where there is a drop in coverage. The cutoffs for this drop in coverage can vary (it can be a 2x drop in coverage or it could be more). Unlike its predacessor software purge_haplotigs, purge_dups can identify whole contigs and also parts of contigs where this is the case.

After purge_dups finds a drop in coverage, it goes ahead and performs a self-BLAST to see if it has some similiarity to some other contig.

If it finds that that portion of genome has similarity to some other contig, then it's going to delete that portion from the original assembly because it knows that this is a retained haplotype.

So by running purge_dups, you are purging out the contigs that are retained haplotigs and you are also purging out parts of your contigs (like tips of your contigs) that there might be haplotypes retained.

The Hifiasm assembler has an internal purging, but it is not perfect for highly heterozygous species, so even for Hifiasm which doesn't require you to have a genome size estimate, you still need to have an idea of your estimated genome size for quality control purposes to have an idea if you're dealing with retained haplotypes. The Darwin Tree of Life project runs purge_dups for every single assembly they run with Hifiasm (their main assembler). Then they perform QC using Merqury plots to see if purge_dups has done a good job.

How do you perform a QC to see if your assembly is well purged? How do you know if you've over-purged and thrown valuable pieces of your genome away? One way to do that is by performing another k-mer analysis using Merqury.

What Merqury basically does is that it takes the k-mers of you assembly and counts them. It will also count the k-mers of your initial reads (instead of counting k-mers with Jellyfish, k-mers are counted with the meryl program which is a tool inside the merqury program. These k-mers are stored in something called a meryl database). It then compares them looking for the intersection of k-mers and plots them (think of this like a Venn Diagrom. The program looks for k-mers that are in both the assembly and the reads).

So baiscally the question you are trying to answer with Merqury is "are all my k-mers in my assembly also in my reads?". If you have a lot of k-mers in your reads that are not present in your assembly, your assembly might be incomplete because they were not all assembled. If on the other hand, you have a lot of k-mers, extra k-mers, or duplicated k-mers in the assembly, when in reality there was supposed to be only one copy in the assembly because there is only one copy of the region (like for example a homozygous region) then you can quickly see that your assembly might contain retained haplotypes.

Here on the right side, you can see a distribution of k-mers in a concatenated primary and alternate assembly. k-mer's either appear 0 times in the assembly (only in the reads), 1 time (k-mers represeting heterozygous regions), 2 times (representing homozygous regions), or 3 or more times (representing repeats). k-mers that appear 1 or more times can also represent k-mers found only in the assembly, although these will be plotted at extremly low coverages, so they will map to the bottom left of the plot and are an easy way to see if there are any errors in the assembly.

Below is an example of what a Merqury plot looks like for a diploid organism's primary assembly only.

You can notice that despite being a diploid organism, this Merqury plot only has 1 times coverage of the k-mers. This is because when plotting only a primary assembly, every region of the genome is only plotted once. The haplotypes are absent from this plot. You can see that there is a lot of coverage of k-mers in where the heterozygous peak would be and those are classified as "read only". These k-mers are not actually "read only", but rather the missing k-mers that are found in the alternate assembly.

You can also kind of see a little bit of green above the homozygous peak at ~70 times coverage. This is indicative of retained haplotypes in the primary assembly. However, this amount of retention is fine. You're hardly ever going to get perfect genome assembly and this is enough to qualify as a high-quality primary assembly.

The last thing to note about Merqury is that in addition to k-mer spectra plots, Merqury will also output alignment stats to give you QV (consensus quality value) estimates in files titled output.qv. QV is an assembly quality metric, based on the number of k-mers that are found only in the assembly but not in the reads (which would imply consensus base pair errors). This is also known as the k-mer survival rate (i.e., how many k-mers are unaffected by sequencing error). To calculate this, the error rate is first determined. The proportion of unique k-mers in the assembly over the total number of k-mers in the assembly represent the error rate, as they are assumed to have base errors. Then, it is recorded as a log-scaled probability of error for the consensus base calls (Phred Quality Score). The formula for the error rate, with E being error rate is:

QV = - 10 ⁢ log E

This means that Q30 corresponds to 99.90% accuracy, Q40 to 99.99%, etc.

Assembly QV used to be estimated based on mapping based techniques. The problem with these however was that mapping biases occured because you have heterozygosity compounded with errors. You expect each read to map back to their specific haplotypes, especially if they are long reads, however this isn't always the case. Complicated regions, like repetative regions and ribosomal RNA arrays can have multiple mappings and supplemental mappings, and these skew the QV estimates. Some studies exclude these regions and QV estimates can be biased or cherry-picked for better results. k-mer-based QV estimation removes a lot of these priors and offers a more accurate estimate quality of the assembled genomes.

If you are ever insecure of your results, it's often a good idea to use multiple approaches to check the sanity of your assumptions. Be aware of the benefits and short-comings of every method and use every tool at your disposal.

One more thing to note on performing genome assembly QC is that you will often see QUAST used to perform QC on multiple assemblies. However, QUAST is reference-based. We live in a time where we are getting quite high resolution with the genomes we produce, which means we are going to have variations. We are going to be able to pick up the total population of variations between the genomes. So if you have a very close sample or the same organism you have sequenced and you want to use QUAST to evaluate because you are just changing the sequencing technology, you can use QUAST and use the reference as a ground truth. However if you have another specimen sequenced, then the QUAST evaluation will be affected by the biology as well. For example, if you take version 38 of the human genome and compare it to the telomere-to-telomere human genome assembly, QUAST is going to give you an output with 20,000 errors on the telomere-to-telomere assembly. However a big part of this is because the assemblies were generated on different human beings with different variation and our technology is able to pick these up very well. Therefore it's always important to keep reference bias aware in your mind and know which tools are reference-based and which are reference-free and also keep in mind that the reference doesn't mean that it presents the ground-truth of the biology of the organisms. Genome assemblies are often mosiacs and heuristics to model certain realities in collected from bulk samples in nature.

Step 5: Proximity Ligation and Chromosome Conformation Capture

It's important to remember that in nature, genomes are organized into a highly organized condensed structure made up of DNA, RNA, and proteins that forms the chromosomes in the nucleus of a cell. This structure is known as chromatin. Chromatin is formed when long strands of DNA molecules wrap around spool-like complexes of proteins called histones to form structures that resemble beads on a string called nucleosomes (at least in most organisms).

This 3D organization is important for gene expression dynamics, but it is also very useful for aiding in assembling genomes. Even though the 3D organization of chromosomes in a nucleus is fluid and constantly changing, there are certain patterns that emerge in DNA organizaiton that can be use to help resolve which regions should be next with other regions. Sometimes distant linear regions of the same chromosome or of other chromosomes tend to stay in close proximity with other distant regions of the genome and this is often done to regulate gene expression. In fact on average, intrachromosomal contact probability is much higher than interchromosomal contact. In other words, Chromosomes are more likely to make contact with themselves than with other chromosomes. Another important pattern is that interaction probability rapidly decays with increasing genomic distance.

And additionally, smaller chromosomes tend to have more interchromosomal contacts than larger chromosomes. Below is a contact matrix of the human chromosomes mapped to themselves. Without going into too much detail, perfect mapping would be right across the grey diagonal region of the heatmap, with no background signal. However, the background signal indicates reads that are also mapping to other parts of the genome in addition to themsevles. You can see that the smaller chromosomes have a higher intensity of off-target mappings.

Capturing this information is very useful for resolving gaps and breaks in genome assembly's as contigs can be scaffolded. Long distance interactions can help orient contigs and join them together in assemblies.

This idea is also very useful when performing metagenome assemblies, where the pool of fragmented DNA is even messier. Long distance DNA interactions captured from the same organisms really help to serve as scaffolds for assembling the pool of DNA from a mixture of organisms.

In recent history, there have been many Chromatin Capture techniques that have been developped, each with their benefits and limitations. The biggest revolution came from development of the Hi-C technique, which allowed for interactions from all regions of the genomes to be screened against all regions from the genome for the first time. A great review about the development of protein capture techniques and their limitations can be found here.

Hi-C works by the following steps:

  1. Cells are first treated with a chemical crosslinker (usually formaldehyde) to freeze interactions between DNA regions that are physically close to each other in the 3D space of the nucleus. This preserves the spatial relationships between different parts of the genome.
  2. The crosslinked DNA is then cut into smaller fragments using restriction enzymes. These enzymes recognize specific DNA sequences and cleave the DNA at those sites, creating smaller, more manageable pieces.
  3. The ends of the DNA fragments are repaired and labeled with a small detectable molecule called biotin. This step ensures that the ends of the DNA fragments are compatible for ligation and can be later captured for sequencing.
  4. The DNA fragments are then ligated under dilute conditions, which favors the joining of DNA fragments that are physically close to each other in the nucleus. This step creates chimeric DNA molecules that represent interactions between different regions of the genome.
  5. The crosslinks are reversed, freeing the DNA from proteins and other molecules. This step also breaks the bonds formed by the crosslinker, which allows the DNA to be processed further.
  6. The DNA is purified and then sheared into smaller fragments, which are more suitable for sequencing.
  7. The biotinylated DNA fragments are captured using streptavidin beads, which bind tightly to biotin. This step enriches for the chimeric DNA molecules that represent interactions between different genomic regions.
  8. The captured DNA fragments are used to prepare a sequencing library (usually as short read-paired ends using Illumina sequencing).
    • Paired ends can be thousands of bases apart on a linear DNA sequence.
  9. The sequencing data is analyzed to map the interactions between different regions of the genome.

The result of Hi-C Chromatin capture data is is a contact matrix (or a contact heatmap), where each entry represents the frequency of interactions between two genomic loci. Different micro-architectures in the 3D conformation of the genome (e.g. chromatin loops, topologically associating domains (TADs), and sub-TADs) will have different contact signatures on the contact matrix.

Although Hi-C has been used as the "Gold-standard" for chromosome conformation capture technology, researchers began reaching the limits of its ability to capture fine-resolution (~1 kb) architectures. Newer technologies such as Micro-C have been able to increase the resolution of chromatin conformation capture. You can see in the plot less gaps in contact (white crosses) appearing when the sample is analyzed using Micro-C instead of Hi-C. This is incredibly useful for epigenetic studies, however for the purposes of genome assembly, these higher resolution standards might be overkill as collecting this data may be very expensive and the Hi-C data is often sufficient for scaffolding a genome.

Nanopore sequencing based chromatin capture techniques are also being developped, such as Pore-C and HiPore-C. These techinques are very promising because they offer similar chromatin capture accuracy as Hi-C, however they claim to offer cheaper and streamlined data collection and analysis.

Additionally, there is another promising technique called Omni-C. Omni-C improves upon the Hi-C protocol because it does not depend on restriction enzymes to digest the crosslinked DNA. Instead it uses a sequence-independent endonuclease. This reduces the sequence bias of the overall library because restriction biases can only digest in certain areas of the genome.

To perform this scaffolding, you generally need a scaffolding algorithm. One of the most common Hi-C scaffolding algorithms is called YaHS. Reduced to it's most basic form, this algorithm basically just takes all the mappings from the paired-end reads and tries to find the regions that have the most mappings. Remember that when mapping softwares do the mapping, there is often a primary mapping as well as many supplemental mappings with lower confidence for each portion of mapped DNA.

Hi-C maps are great for finding misassemblies in certain contigs or scaffolds. These sequences should have strange distribution of Hi-C mappings within their sequences and would likely need manual fixing.

Another genome assembly phenomenon that you can encounter are imposter sequences. These are sequences that somehow got incorporated to your contigs or scaffolds but will share no mappings with any other sequences.

Another thing you might encounter is retained haplotigs in your assemblies. Even after using purge_dups to try and remove these, some of these assembly artificats can bypass detection by the software and still persist in your genome. The Hi-C maps can quickly identify them and allow you to fix them manually.

Hi-C maps can also give you a good indication of the completeness of your genomes because (in probably most eukaryotic organisms), you should see a nice, repetative, off-diagonal mapping pattern accross the middle and ends of all of your final chromosomes, indicating the shared similarity of centromeric and telomeric sequences across chromosomes.

There are many more ways Hi-C can help understand your genome and it is too much to all list here. More information on how to interpret Hi-C maps can be found in this document.

One good way to think of Hi-C maps is as modern day Karyotypes. They can quickly show you the number of- and size of the chromosomes in an organisms genome. But in addition, they can quickly reveal a lot of other genomic patterns and unique characteristics of your organism's biology.

We tend to take certain things in biology for granted: "Centromeres are very repetative", "Telomeres are organized in specific ways", etc. Now with these chromatin-capture technologies we are able to do this high-resolution biology that we were never able to do before. We can now easily evaluate how our previous biological notions hold up across the tree of life.

Scaffolding using some sort of chromatin-capture technology is a very important ingredient of creating a high-quality genome assembly. Not only for the myriads of benefits of having better data for identifying contamination, misassemblies, and genome features like sex chromosomes, but also just on a basic level whereby proximity-ligation-based scaffolding can significantly improve the contiguity of your genome.

Here you can see an example where just by performing Hi-C scaffolding on an assembly using short-reads, the researchers were able to go from a contig N50 of 64,321 to a scaffold N50 of 140,950,122. This is a over 2000-fold increase in genome contiguity.

After running a scaffolding software, there genome will still likely not be complete and will need some human intervention for refinement. This is called manual curation. Manual curation should be done using PretextView. This is an app that allows inspection of Hi-C reads mapped to an assembly.

PretextView can be used to make real-time changes to assemblies:

  1. It can be used to break and join scaffolds.
  2. It can be used to re-orient scaffolds or parts thereof.
  3. Additional tracks (bedgraphs) can be injected into Pretext maps to view alongside the Hi-C data, e.g., Coverage, Telomeres, Sequence gaps, Repeat density.

PretextView can also be used to dump an agp (describes the assembly of a larger sequence object from smaller objects) of changes to an assembly. This agp can then be passed to our in-house script rapid_pretext2tpf.py, which will allow the edited map to be turned back into a corrected fasta file.

Some caveats to be aware of are:

  • PretextView is limited by resolution.
  • Mis-assemblies may be hidden in large genomes.
  • Very small scaffolds may not have sufficient resolution to correctly determine placement/orientation.

Step 6: Organelle Genome Assembly

The final thing to take note of is organelle genome assemblies. Every time you isolate DNA from an organism, a large percentage of the reads will come from the organelle genomes. Each organelle (mitochondria and chlroplasts (in photosynthetic eukaryotes)) typically contains multiple copies of their own genome. The number of copies can vary depending on the cell type, organism, and metabolic demands, but as a general rule of thumb for well studied organisms, a single mitochondrion may contain 2 to 10 copies of mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) and a single chloroplast can contain 10 to 100 copies of chloroplast DNA (cpDNA). In addition, a cell can have tenss, to hundreds, to thousands of organelles per cell, the overall number of organellular DNA copies per cell can range from hundreds to tens of thousands.

A lot of assembly programs will output files that end in .gfa. These are Graphical Fragment Assembly files. These files contain information from the graph space of the genome assembly (which is like the intermediate version of the assembly, before the assembly is linearized). A .gfa file consists of a set of sequences and tab-delimited pairs of sequences with their overlaps. You can read more about this format from here. These graphical fragment assembly files can be visualized in programs like Bandage, which help you visualize assembly graphs of your genomes. An assembly graph is used to represent the final assembly of a genome (or metagenomes). In simple terms, the assembler builds this assembly graph based on reads and their overlap information. Then the assembler resolves paths across the assembly graph and outputs non-branching paths as contigs.

Genome assembly graphs in Bandage can be very confusing to interpret at first. I recommend reading the Bandage wiki and watching these videos before trying to work with it.

Here you can see what a typical graph structure of a chloroplast genome assembly looks like.

The way to basically interpret this is as follows

This is an example of a resolved DNA structure. The way to think about this is to imagine tracing a single path through the graph and then linearizing that trace. This results in a single, circular sequence of DNA.

If you think about it, because there are 3 nodes and 4 edges in the above graph, with one of the nodes coming from collapsed repeated sequences, there are 2 ways to traverse the graph above: two separate circular sequences that share a region in common, or a single larger circular sequence with an element that occurs twice.

It's important to always take into account the biology of your organism when interpretting this graph. If you combine this information with additional knowledge about chloroplast structures and how they contain long, inverted repeats, then you can rule out the first scenario and deduce that the real structure of the bacterial genome would be the second option. In this way, Bandage has assisted in turning a fragmented assembly of three contigs into a completed genome of one sequence.

Assembly of the chloroplast genome, especially in plants, is usually a very streamlined process. There are usually no real issues with it and softwares like Oatk are really good at assembling them. However, mitochondrial genome assembly can still be a complicated challenge. Mitochondrial biology can be more diverse than chloroplast biology generally. Here is just a quick rundown of many differences between mitochondria found in plants and animals.

This variety extends past animals and plants. Across the Eukaryotic tree of life there is a wide variety of genetic architecture. You will often find many differences in intergenic regions, number and length of introns, the presence and number of pseudogenes, etc., between organisms.

However despite the differences in structure between mitochondria of different organisms, their genetic function is well conserved across many Eukaryotic organisms (except for in anaerobic eukaryotes whose mitochondria have evolved into hydrogenosomes or mitosomes). There is also no correlation between genome size and gene content

Below is an example of how these genes are layed out on a mitochondrial genome.

It's also important to remember that mitochondria exhibit heteroplasmy, which is the presence of more than one type of mtDNA in an individual. This was thought to be a rare phenomenon that was associated with aging and disease, but with modern sequencing technology we are realising that it is quite common amongst many taxa. Heterplasmy can exist at the level of different mtDNA between cells of an organism but also different mtDNA within the same cell.

Another important concept for mitochondrial genome assembly is NUMTs, which stands for Nuclear Mitochondrial DNA segments. These are created by the transfer of portions of the mitochondrial genome to the nuclear genome. Because this is usually an evolutionary process that takes a lot of time, the original mitochondrial genome that's inserted into the nuclear genome tend to go through mutations over the generations and becomes diversified away from the original sequence. However, there is still some similarity which makes some software and analytical tools that try to find mitochondrial sequences confused and they predict that NUMTs are really the mitochondrial sequences.

Because of all of these peculiarities, specialized softwares to assemble mitochondrial genomes have been developped that take all of these factors into consideration. One of these is MitoHiFi.

As a broad outline, MitoHiFi works as follows:

  1. Assemble the genome
  2. Filter out NUMTs / incomplete assemblies
  3. Remove redundancy
  4. Annotate the genome

More specifically, this is how the MitoHiFi workflow looks like. To run MitoHiFi, you will always need to have a reference genome. This will probably need to be a mitogenome of a closely related organism. The MitoHiFi program comes with a script that helps you find a closely-related reference mitogenome called findMitoReference.py. MitoHiFi can also be run in two different modes: The first one is the reads mode (-r). Here you can input you PacBio HiFi reads or your Nanopore high-quality reads together with the reference mitogenome. Then the reads are mapped to the reference genome and only the reads with high-quality mappings are retained because these reads that likely came from the mitochondrial DNA. These reads are then fed into hifiasm to assemble contigs. The contigs are then aligned to the reference mitogenome, and then only the best aligning contigs are retained. The selected contigs are circularized and then annotated with either MitoFinder or MITOS. You can also run this in contig mode (-c), so if you already have an initial set of contigs then this you just skip the hifiasm assembly and go straight to the BLAST stage.

To run findMitoReference.py, you provide as input the name of your species, your email, an output directory and a minimum mitogenome reference length to ensure that the mitogenome you get back is not an incomplete or partial mitogenome (suggested to set this to around 15000 for an animal mitogenome).

To run the pipeline (using the main mitohifi.py script), provide your reads or contigs using the -r or -c flags, respectively (for the reads you can input multiple .fasta files). You also need to specify the reference mitogenome with the -f flag and also a GenBank file of the reference mitogenome with the -g flag. -t is used to specificy the number of threads to run this program with. You also need to specify the organism's genetic code following the NCBI table using the -o flag (for the mitogenome annotation step). The inputs for this parameter are:

  • 2 - The Vertebrate Mitochondrial Code
  • 3 - The Yeast Mitochondrial Code
  • 4 - The Mold, Protozoan, and Coelenterate Mitochondrial Code and the Mycoplasma/Spiroplasma Code
  • 5 - The Invertebrate Mitochondrial Code
  • 6 - The Ciliate, Dasycladacean and Hexamita Nuclear Code
  • 9 - The Echinoderm and Flatworm Mitochondrial Code
  • 10 - The Euplotid Nuclear Code
  • 11 - The Bacterial, Archaeal and Plant Plastid Code
  • 12 - The Alternative Yeast Nuclear Code
  • 13 - The Ascidian Mitochondrial Code
  • 14 - The Alternative Flatworm Mitochondrial Code
  • 16 - Chlorophycean Mitochondrial Code
  • 21 - Trematode Mitochondrial Code
  • 22 - Scenedesmus obliquus Mitochondrial Code

If you ever need to view the help page or view more options, simply run python mitohifi.py -h.

More information on how MitoHiFi works under the hood is shown below. First, if you are using read mode, reads are mapped to the reference and mapped reads are assembled with hifiasm to produce contigs. These contigs (or the ones you provided in contig mode) are then BLASTed to the reference mitogenome, and then the results of the alignment are parsed and filtered according to three different criterea:

  1. If the length of the query (contig) is 5 times higher than the size of the subject (reference mitogenome), then it is filtered out because this contig likely represents a NUMT, not a mitogenome
  2. If the coverage of the query (contig) is 80% smaller than the length of the subject (reference mitogenome), then it is filtered out because it suggests that your contig is an incomplete or partial mitogenome.
  3. If the percentage of the query (contig) in the BLAST match is smaller than 50%, the contig is filtered out because you don't have strong evidence that this contig really is a mitogenome.

You can tweak all of these parameters from their default values. The most important parameter out of all of these to be aware of is the last one, which you can change with the -p flag. If you are dealing with mitogenomes from taxa that are usually well conserved, like mammals or vertebrates, then you can likely increase this default 50% filter to something higher like 70% or 80%. The default value of 50% was chosen because the Darwin Tree of Life team noticed that for some invertebrate mitogenomes, a lot of their mitogenomes are really dissimilar to each other, especially in the repeated regions, and that sometimes picking a very stringent value would cause the researcher to overfilter real mitogenomes that were just divergent sequence-wise.

Once the pool of contigs are filtered, the program then proceeds to the circularization step. Because of the circular nature of the mitochondrial genome, you will have sequencing reads that support overlaps all across the sequence. So for example, if you look at the diagram below, you will have many reads that support connections from regions A <-> B, B <-> C, and C <-> A. Sometimes, during the assembly process, the assemblers can get confused and you might end up having a linear contig that contains the same sequence at the start and end positions. So sometimes with circular DNA you will get an assembly artifact where you get a contig with redundant information.

To solve this issue, MitoHiFi performs a self-blast on the contigs. If a contig has a redundant region at the beginning and the end, the region at the end is deleted. This is performed using the script inside circularizationCheck.py.

The next thing that MitoHiFi does is rotate the contig. This is to ensure that all the potential mitogenomes have the same start location. The creators of MitoHiFi decided to standardize this so at the mitogenome sequence starts that the tRNA-Phe coding gene. However, in order rotate the sequence so that it starts at this gene, the contig has to be annotated first, The mitogenome is annotated using the GenBank information of the reference mitogenome.

The last step in the MitoHiFi pipeline is to take all the potential mitogenomes that have remained passed the previous filters, and then choose the final mitogenome. The final mitogenome is that one that satisfies these three criteria:

  1. The genome is circularized
  2. The genome is similar to the reference mitogenome (in overall size)
  3. There are no frameshifts in the annotated genes.

Once MitoHifi finishes running, you expect to have these 3 main files as outputs: final_mitogenome.fasta, final_mitogenome.gb, and final_mitogenome.annotation.png. If you are running MitoHifi in the reads mode (-r), you will also have another important file called final_mitogenome.coverage.png. This is a graph that shows the sequencing coverage of the reads. This is a sanity check to see if there could have been any drops in coverage across the genome that could have caused a misassembly of the final mitogenome. You will likely see a drop in coverage at the beginning and the end of the mitogenome but this is okay. This is a common artifact of a mitogenome assembly

However there are also more files that allow you to perform a more in-depth analysis MitoHiFi. These files let you view details about the other contigs that made it to the last step of the pipeline but were not selected as the final mitogenome (other potential mitogenomes). shared_genes.tsv is also a useful output to check the similarity between your final mitogenome and the mitogenome used as the reference.

And also there are these files that in most cases are probably too much information but are present regardless if you need to investigate further.

An alternative to MitoHiFi is Oatk. This program is especially useful for chloropast genomes, but can also be use for assembling mitogenomes as well.

A final note on mitogenome assembly is that organelle genome assembly is a thing that is kept in mind during the entire genome assembly process. For example, if you want to run MitoHiFi in contig mode, the best time do so would be right after you perform the initial assembly step. This is because other programs in the assembly pipeline like purge_dups might purge out the mitogenome contigs because the mitochondria has a different coverage to the nuclear genome. For the Darwin Tree of Life Genome Assembly pipeline specifically, there is another step that was not really touched on here which is contamination filtering before manual curation. They have their own pipeline that uses blobtoolkit and they use that to find mitochondrial contigs and other organelle contigs that they then remove. The organelle genomes are then assembled seperated from the nuclear genomes at the end. The final genome .fasta files that are deposited in databases like the NCBI will have the organelle genomes concatenated to the end of the file but it's important to be aware that these are all assembled seperately.
